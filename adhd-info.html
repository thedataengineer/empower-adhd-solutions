ADHD, Explained

ADHD is consistently inconsistent. It looks different in every person who has it, which is part of why getting diagnosed can be so hard.

What is ADHD?

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition tied to how the brain regulates dopamine and norepinephrine, the chemical messengers that carry signals between brain regions responsible for attention, motivation, and self-regulation. When those signals run low, the brain struggles to direct attention on demand, even when the stakes are high and the intention is real.

ADHD rarely travels alone. Depression, anxiety, and other conditions frequently come along with it, which can mask the ADHD itself and complicate diagnosis.

Common ADHD traits

Not every trait below appears in the DSM-5, but clinicians and people with ADHD recognize all of them. You do not need all twelve to have ADHD.

  • Poor working memory
  • Emotional dysregulation
  • Time blindness
  • Hyperfocus
  • Difficulty starting tasks
  • Inconsistent attention
  • Impulsivity
  • Restlessness
  • Disorganization
  • Forgetfulness in daily activities
  • Rejection sensitivity
  • Trouble finishing what you start

Different, not deficient

ADHD is a neurological difference, not a broken version of a normal brain. The same wiring that makes Tuesday-morning paperwork miserable also tends to produce creativity, adaptability, and the ability to lock in completely on problems that matter. The goal is not to fix your brain; it is to build a life that fits it.

Getting evaluated as an adult

Untreated ADHD in adults commonly shows up as chronic underperformance at work, strained relationships, money problems, and a running internal monologue about wasted potential. If that sounds familiar, a professional evaluation is worth it, a diagnosis unlocks treatment options and, for many adults, a profound reframe of their whole history.

A comprehensive evaluation typically includes a clinical interview covering your history back to childhood, standardized rating scales, psychological testing, and sometimes a medical exam to rule out other causes. Look for a psychologist, psychiatrist, or clinic that explicitly lists adult ADHD evaluation as a specialty; the Resources page has places to start.

What a diagnosis requires

Broadly, a clinician is looking for symptoms that have been present since childhood, that show up in more than one area of life (work, home, relationships), and that cause real impairment, not just occasional distraction. Everyone loses their keys sometimes; ADHD is when the pattern is lifelong and it costs you.
